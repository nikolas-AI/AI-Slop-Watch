Growth & Statistics

The numbers behind the AI content explosion, translated into plain English, so you can see exactly what's happening.

From a trickle to a flood, in just three years

In January 2022, AI-generated content on the internet was negligible, almost like a statistical rounding error. Then ChatGPT launched in late November 2022. In the twelve months that followed, the percentage of AI-written articles shot from near-zero to nearly 39% of all published articles.

To put that in human terms: imagine a busy newsroom where 10 reporters file stories every day. Then suddenly, 6 of those 10 desks are replaced by machines that never sleep, never ask for a paycheck, and never slow down. That's what happened to the internet's content supply chain.

Plain English Takeaway By the end of 2023, roughly 4 out of every 10 articles you might encounter online was written by an AI, not a human. Researchers predict this could reach 9 out of 10 by end of 2026.

It's not just articles, it's everything

The growth of synthetic content goes far beyond text. AI tools are now flooding every type of media on the internet. The volume of deepfake videos jumped from around 500,000 in 2023 to approximately 8 million in 2025, a 16x increase in just two years.

Images tell an even more staggering story: researchers estimate that more than 34 million AI-generated images are created every single day. Since 2022, more than 15 billion synthetic images have been produced in total.

Human Content vs. AI Content: A Side-by-Side Look

Not all AI content is dangerous. But understanding the key differences between human-created and AI-generated content helps us see why the flood of synthetic material is such a problem for trust and authenticity.

Visual 3 — Human vs. AI Content Comparison

Feature ✅ Human-Written Content ⚠ AI-Generated Content
Time to produce Hours to days of research and writing Seconds (no research required)
Fact-checking Editors verify facts before publishing Often none. AI can confidently state false "facts"
Accountability Author and publication are identifiable Often anonymous or falsely attributed
Motivation Inform, persuade, entertain with intent Often optimized for clicks, engagement, or deception
Volume Limited by human capacity Essentially unlimited (scales to millions per day)
Accuracy Generally verified or clearly labeled as opinion Frequently contains confident-sounding errors ("hallucinations")
Detectability Comes with byline, style, credentials Designed to look exactly like real human writing

TABLE 1 — A comparison of key characteristics between human-created and AI-generated content, highlighting the specific risks that synthetic content introduces to digital information spaces.
