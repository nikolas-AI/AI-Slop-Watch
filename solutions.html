Page 05 — The Path Forward

Solutions & What Can Be Done

The problem is real and serious, but it is not hopeless. Here's what researchers, technology companies, and everyday people can do to push back.

There is reason for hope, but it requires action

The flood of AI-generated content is a real crisis, but crises have solutions. Researchers, governments, technology companies, and civic organizations are all working on different pieces of the puzzle. No single fix will work on its own, but together, these approaches can meaningfully slow the tide and rebuild trust in online information.

The Expert Consensus Matt Groh, an assistant professor at Northwestern University who researches deepfake detection, says the best near-term approach combines two things: better detection technology and broader public awareness. Until the technology catches up, awareness is our best defense. (Source: Brady / Kellogg Insight, 2023)

Three levels of solutions

Level 01 — Technology

Building Better Detection and Authentication Systems

The most powerful long-term solution is technology that can detect AI content automatically and verify the origin of legitimate content. This is where most serious investment is happening.

Content Provenance Standards

Organizations like the C2PA are building technical standards that embed invisible "certificates of authenticity" into legitimate content, like a digital watermark that travels with a photo or video wherever it goes.

AI Detection Software

Researchers are building tools that analyze text, images, and audio to flag likely AI-generated content. These tools are improving rapidly, though they're still in an arms race with increasingly sophisticated generators.

Platform-Level Labeling

Major platforms like Meta, YouTube, and X/Twitter are implementing labels that automatically flag AI-generated or AI-assisted content, similar to how social media already labels satire or paid advertising.

Level 02 — Policy & Law

Regulation, Disclosure, and Accountability

Technology alone isn't enough. Laws and policies set the rules of the road, what's allowed, what must be disclosed, and what happens when those rules are broken.

  • Mandatory disclosure laws: Several U.S. states and the European Union have passed or are developing laws requiring disclosure when AI was used to create political advertising, news content, or other high-stakes material.
  • Deepfake criminalization: Some jurisdictions now treat non-consensual deepfakes of individuals as a criminal offense, particularly when used for harassment or sexual exploitation.
  • Platform liability reform: Policy debates are ongoing about whether social media platforms should bear more legal responsibility for hosting and amplifying AI-generated misinformation.
  • International cooperation: Because AI misinformation is global in scale, solutions require governments to work together on shared standards and enforcement mechanisms.

Level 03 — Public Awareness

What You Can Do Right Now

While waiting for technology and policy to catch up, the most powerful tool available is an informed public. Being aware of the problem is itself a defense against it. Here's how to protect yourself and others.

  • Pause before you share. Ask: where did this come from? Does this claim seem too surprising or perfectly designed to make me angry? Those are signals to verify before sharing.
  • Check original sources. If an article makes a striking claim, look for the original source, not the article itself, but the underlying data, quote, or event it references.
  • Look for provenance signals. When you see a photo or video, look for labels from platforms, publishers, or tools like the C2PA that certify where a piece of content came from.
  • Use reverse image search. Tools like Google Images or TinEye can tell you where a photo has appeared before, a simple way to spot repurposed or manipulated images.
  • Be skeptical of emotional urgency. AI-generated misinformation is often designed to provoke strong emotional reactions, outrage, fear, or excitement. Strong emotion is a signal to slow down.

Spotlight: The C2PA and Content Authentication

The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) is one of the most significant institutional efforts to fight AI-generated misinformation at a technical level. Founded by major technology companies including Adobe, Microsoft, Intel, and BBC, the C2PA is building open technical standards that answer a simple but critical question: Where did this content come from?

Think of it like nutrition labels on food packaging. Before those labels existed, you had to take a manufacturer's word for what was in your food. C2PA is building the equivalent of nutrition labels for digital content, an embedded, tamper-resistant certificate that travels with an image, video, or article and tells you exactly how it was created, who published it, and whether it has been modified.

When these standards are widely adopted, a reader or viewer could check the "label" on any piece of content and know whether it was created by a verified journalist, generated by an AI tool, or modified after its original creation.

How C2PA Works

  • Content is created and an invisible cryptographic certificate is attached
  • The certificate records who made it, when, and with what tools
  • Certificate travels with the content across the internet
  • Viewers and platforms can verify the certificate instantly
  • Tampering with the content breaks the certificate, a visible red flag

Why Awareness Matters Right Now Detection technology and policy solutions take years to develop and deploy. In the meantime, the most effective defense is a public that knows the problem exists, understands how it works, and knows to pause before trusting and sharing information it encounters online. That's why this website exists.

The Bottom Line

The internet is worth saving.

Reliable information is foundational to how modern democratic societies function. We make decisions about health, politics, safety, and relationships based on what we read and see. When that information environment is flooded with synthetic fakes, the entire system of shared reality we depend on is at risk.

The good news is that we are not powerless. Understanding the problem is the first step. Supporting organizations that are building solutions, like the C2PA, is the second. Using our own judgment carefully every time we read, share, and believe something online is the third.
